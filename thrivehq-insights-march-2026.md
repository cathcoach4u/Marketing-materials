## 1. Themes this week

**AI as an ADHD tool — and its limits**
Members shared real experiences of AI changing how they work and solve problems. The key insight: AI removes the "wall of text" problem that makes Google overwhelming for ADHD brains. It gives answers in normal language, step by step, without mining through forums. Several members described genuine productivity wins. At the same time, the coach raised an important counter-theme — AI-generated communication strips out warmth and personality, and real human connection is something no tool can replace. ThriveHQ was named directly as a space that offers what AI cannot.

**Body doubling — in families, not just at desks**
A member described using body doubling with a family member to tackle a long-avoided, overwhelming task. There was no judgment, no conflict, just presence and working alongside. The result was better than expected. The phrase that emerged: "I won't do it for you, but I'll do it with you." This is a practical, warm framing of a well-known ADHD concept that has clear everyday applications beyond the office.

**The procrastination-to-completion gap**
Multiple members recognised the pattern of avoiding a task for days, then completing it in a fraction of the expected time. The emotional experience after completing it — relief, self-acknowledgement, feeling "a heap better" — was consistent. This is one of the most universally relatable ADHD experiences and came up with real specificity this session.

**Scaffolding looks different for different strengths profiles**
The session explored how different members need different scaffolding. High adaptability needs anchoring points and flexible blocks, not rigid daily habits. High responsibility and consistency can be channelled into follow-through, but also needs recentering tools. High empathy and relator need depth in relationships to feel regulated. There is no one-size-fits-all structure — and ThriveHQ is where members figure out what works for them.

**Emotional dysregulation and the shame that follows**
A member shared being visibly dysregulated in front of a professional and feeling embarrassed and judged. The coach held this carefully — naming the dysregulation, exploring the shame, and connecting it back to the ADHD experience. This theme of shame after losing control is one of the most common but least openly discussed ADHD experiences in adults, particularly parents.
