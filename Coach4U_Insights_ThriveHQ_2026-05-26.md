**Session:** 26 May 2026 | Module: ThriveHQ (ADHD Group Coaching) | All content anonymised

## 1. Themes This Week

**AI as a personalised ADHD support tool**
The session explored how members are using AI tools in ways that go beyond general productivity. One member described loading their own strengths profile into their AI system so that every response is tailored to how they actually think and execute. Another member worked with the coach in real time to build a personalised study anchor prompt using their specific strengths as the design constraints. This is a practical, concrete demonstration of strengths-based coaching applied through technology, and it positions ThriveHQ as a place where members learn to build tools that fit their brain.

**Replacing dopamine, not removing it**
A significant coaching reframe emerged around phone use and distraction. Rather than telling a member to put the phone away, the coach challenged this approach directly, arguing that the ADHD brain needs a dopamine source to function and removing it without replacement simply does not work. The alternative: find something engaging enough to compete with it. NotebookLM's audio podcast feature was demonstrated live as a potential replacement, turning study notes into a conversation between two speakers. This is a counterintuitive, practical insight that has strong content appeal.

**Anchoring systems built around the individual, not a template**
The session revisited the concept of anchoring points as a structure that works with ADHD rather than against it. Members explored what anchoring looks like for their specific situation, study commitments, energy patterns, and strength profiles. The coaching moved away from prescribing a system and toward helping each person identify their own version of structure. The phrase that captured it well was the idea of a "good day plan and a bad day plan," a simple scaffold that acknowledges that not every day is the same.

**The ideation loop and learning to stay the course**
A member described the experience of a relentless inner voice cycling through career possibilities, always suggesting that something more important or more meaningful is just around the corner. The coaching held space for this without dismissing it, and explored how to distinguish between genuine purpose and the ADHD brain generating options. The practical anchor: you do not need to make all the decisions now. You need one system that gets you to the next step.

**What sustained progress actually looks like**
One member described feeling that their cup had been filled in all directions, that they were not missing out on anything, and that they felt genuinely content with where things were. This came after a period of active coaching. It is a quiet but significant proof point for what the program is designed to produce.

## 3. Proof Points

- A member has integrated their strengths profile into their AI setup so that responses are automatically tailored to their thinking style and execution preferences. They reported this has been genuinely useful for navigating challenges in their daily life.
- A coach and member built a personalised study anchor plan together in real time during the session, using the member's strengths profile as the design input. The member left with a working prompt they can use immediately.
- A member passed a demanding university subject during a particularly difficult period in their personal life, completing something they described as weighing on them for the full semester.
- One member described feeling content, connected, and with a full sense of purpose across work, family, and community. This was attributed to sustained effort over the coaching period.
- Members are actively sharing tools and strategies with each other inside the session, not just receiving them from the coach. The group dynamic is functioning as peer support as well as coached learning.

## 5. Marketing Action This Week

Write a short LinkedIn post built around the phrase "don't put the phone away, replace what it gives you." Open by naming the moment: sitting down to study or work, the phone keeps appearing in your hand, and you feel like you have no willpower. Then reframe it: the ADHD brain is not being lazy. It is looking for something that gives it what it needs to stay engaged. The post should end with the practical insight from the session: the goal is not to remove the distraction, but to find something more engaging to compete with it. Connect this to what ThriveHQ offers: a place to find the strategies that actually fit your brain, not generic advice that requires willpower you do not have. This post speaks directly to ADHD adults who are tired of being told to try harder, which is a large part of the ThriveHQ audience.
