## 1. Themes This Week

**AI as a practical tool for ADHD brains**
A significant theme this session was the role of AI in reducing the friction that ADHD brains experience with traditional research and information overload. Members described how AI removes the "wall of text" problem common when searching online, giving step-by-step answers in plain language without requiring the user to mine through forums or filter irrelevant information. This is directly relevant to how ThriveHQ positions its own value: working with how the brain actually functions, not against it.

**The gap between human connection and digital communication**
The session surfaced a meaningful tension between AI-assisted communication and authentic human connection. The coach shared a personal experience of receiving messages that felt impersonal and automated, and described the emotional impact of that loss of warmth. The group explored how genuine relationship and community cannot be replicated by tools alone. This framing positions ThriveHQ's group format as something distinctly valuable: a real human space in an increasingly automated world.

**Body doubling as a family strategy**
Members explored body doubling not just as a workplace productivity technique but as something that works naturally within family relationships. The phrase that captured it well was the idea of doing something alongside someone rather than for them. This practical reframe of a well-known ADHD concept opens up new ways to talk about how ThriveHQ members are applying what they learn beyond the group sessions.

**The procrastination-to-completion gap**
Multiple members recognised a pattern of avoiding a task for days, then completing it in a fraction of the expected time. The relief and self-acknowledgement that followed was consistent and notable. This is one of the most universally relatable ADHD experiences and was discussed with specificity and humour, making it a strong content opportunity.

**Scaffolding that fits your brain, not a generic system**
The session explored how different members need different structures. High adaptability benefits from anchoring points and flexible blocks rather than rigid daily habits. Other strength profiles benefit from non-negotiables and alternating rhythms. The coaching reinforced that there is no one-size-fits-all system, and that ThriveHQ is where members find what actually works for them.

## 3. Proof Points

- A member used the weekly Brain Pulse to ask "what do I need right now?" during a low-energy moment, pivoted their approach, and recovered their focus and productivity before the end of the day
- A member applied body doubling with a family member to tackle a long-avoided task, with no conflict or judgment, and achieved a result neither had managed alone
- A member recognised and named their own emotional dysregulation in the moment, rather than pushing through and crashing afterward
- A member tackled a task they had been avoiding for days, completed it within an hour, and self-acknowledged the effort, which they identified as a change from their previous pattern
- A member used their strengths to name their needs directly in a professional relationship, which de-escalated tension and created genuine understanding
- Members were applying ADHD-informed language, including dysregulation, scaffolding, and body doubling, naturally within their own family systems without prompting from the coach

## 5. Marketing Action This Week

Write and publish a short LinkedIn post centred on the phrase "I'll do it with you, not for you." Open with the common experience of staring at an overwhelming task and not knowing where to start. Explain body doubling in two or three sentences using plain, non-clinical language. Then reframe it for everyday life — with a partner, a child, a friend, or a colleague — not just in a productivity context. Close by connecting it to what ThriveHQ offers: a group that shows up alongside members each week, not to do the work for them, but to make starting possible. This post will resonate strongly with ADHD adults who are parents or in relationships, which is a large segment of ThriveHQ's potential audience.
